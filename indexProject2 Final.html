Quentin Tarantino’s 1994 film Pulp Fiction, tells the stories of multiple characters in anon-linear timeline, who cross paths with each other throughout the film. Among those characters is a man named Butch, played by Bruce Willis. He is a boxer who got payed by the gangster Marsellus Wallace to lose his upcoming boxing match. However he decides not to go through with it. The sequence to be analyzed is the day after the fight. Butch is hiding out in a motel with his girlfriend Fabienne, knowing that Marsellus’ people will be looking for him. They’re talking about the breakfast they want to have just before hitting the road, as Butch looks for a watch in his suitcase. He searches frantically, throwing stuff around the room, yelling at Fabienne asking her if she was sure to have packed the watch; she says she did, but the watch is nowhere to be found. This sequence is about the significance an object can have on a person. Most importantly, it shows how masculinity affects Butch.

Science fiction can be an extremely underrated genre. People tend to disregard it as not serious enough, not universal enough, or just mediocre entertainment. People look down on stories about made-up planets and alien species, deeming that they’re not relatable. However, it seems to me that these alien stories can sometimes portray humanity even better than other non- science fiction stories. After all, that is what science fiction movies do; “[they] reflect world- wide anxieties, and [...] allay them” (Sontag, 42). This is one of the arguments that Susan Sontag makes in her 1965 essay The Imagination of Disaster. Sontag believes that fantasy stories have two possible effects on their audience: They can serve as an escape and/or distraction from our regular lives, or they can actually “normalize what is psychologically unbearable” (Sontag, 42). In her essay, Sontag also breaks down three sorts of formulas that science fiction movies tend to follow. Even if these typical examples were based on movies before 1965, they are still completely applicable to the science fiction movies we see today.

Radio Raheem and Buggin’ Out come in blasting loud music and screaming at Sal, and this is all communicated by the choice of framing. The fact that it is shot from a canted angle and in close-ups signifies the anger and disfunction present in the characters and it unsettles the viewer during this aggressive exchange. Another interesting thing to point out is the tilt on that canted angle as well. It appears that Radio Raheem and Buggin’ Out are shot from a slightly lower angle than Sal; this is to show that, at least initially, Sal is the one with less power since he is being looked down on.
